Hypertension


    Introduction

    • Abnormally high blood pressure (BP)
    • No clear-cut demarcation between normal and abnormal BP
    • In 1958, WHO defined high blood pressure (HBP) as BP > 165/95
    • Today, BP ≥ 140/90 is regarded as high

    Types

    • Systemic (Arterial)
    • Portal
    • Intracranial
    • Ocular (Glaucoma)

    Epidemiology

    Health Burden of CVDs

    • 20th century: CVD responsible for fewer than 10% of all deaths worldwide.
    • About 30 percent today.
    • 80% of CVD deaths occur in low- and middle-income countries.
    • Assuming 25% of Nigerians are hypertensive.
    • Over 40 million Nigerians.
    • Middle and top-level workforce seriously affected.
    • Implications for national productivity and economy.
    • Hypertension accounts for 30 percent of clinic visits for people aged 45 to 64 years (Ogunniyi).
    • For people of working age (45‚Äì64 years), this translates to 1,021 visits per 1,000 individuals (Cherry 2008).
    • A quarter of Emergency ward admission is hypert-related (Ekere).

    Clinical Features

    • Usually no complaint
    • Symptoms suggest complications
    • Commonly associated with sudden events such as stroke, heart attack, and sudden death.
    • The only way to know is to check BP

    Symptoms

    • Asymptomatic
    • Headache
    • Poor sleep
    • Blurring of vision
    • Epistaxis
    • Chest pain
    • Breathlessness
    • Palpitations
    • Convulsions

    Diagnosis

    • BP measurement using sphyg after 5-10 min rest
    • BP > 140/90 on two occasions, two weeks apart
    • Thickened arterial wall
    • Displaced apex beat
    • Loud A2
    • Fundoscopy- hypertensive retinopathy

    Routine Investigation

    • Urinalysis
    • Serum creatinine and electrolytes
    • Blood glucose
    • Blood lipid profile
    • Electrocardiogram

    Complications of Systemic Hypertension

    Complications of systemic hypertension can occur:

    1. Acutely, i.e. develop suddenly over a few hours or days
    2. Chronically, i.e. develop over several months to years. The result of this may also present suddenly

    Complications

    • Heart Failure
    • TIA
    • Stroke
    • Peripheral vascular dx
    • CKD or CRF
    • Malignant Hypertension
    • Eclampsia

    Complications of Systemic Hypertension

    1. Acute Hypertensive Encephalopathy: This is a constellation of symptoms that occur when the BP rises to very high levels and causes a disturbance of normal brain function. This manifests as severe headache, vomiting, convulsions, blurred vision, and loss of consciousness.
    2. Myocardial Infarction: Occurs when the blood supply to the heart muscle is blocked.
    3. Heart Failure: When the heart cannot pump enough blood to satisfy the body‚Äôs needs.
    4. Stroke: Occurs when a blood vessel supplying the brain ruptures or is blocked by a blood clot. This is due to prior damage of the vessel wall by hypertension.
    5. Kidney Disease: Hypertension can cause kidney damage either acutely when blood pressure rises to very high levels suddenly or after several years of poor BP control.
    6. Hypertensive Retinopathy: Uncontrolled hypertension damages the blood vessels in the eyes and can lead to blindness.
    7. Peripheral Vascular Disease: This occurs when the body‚Äôs blood vessels are damaged by hypertension. It can lead to hand or leg ulcers and eventual limb amputation.

    Important Clinical Scenarios

    Resistant Hypertension: Causes

    • Nonadherence
    • Inadequate doses
    • Inappropriate combinations
    • Drugs: NSAID, Cocaine, amphetamines
    • Sympathomimetics (decongestants, anorectics)
    • Obesity
    • Excess alcohol intake
    • Contraceptives
    • Erythropoetin
    • Secondary causes of hypertension
    • Improper BP measurement
    • OTC interactions
    • Inadequate diuresis

    Hypertensive Urgency

    • Encephalopathy
    • SAH (Subarachnoid hemorrhage)
    • Dissecting aortic aneurysm
    • Pulmonary Edema
    • AMI (Acute Myocardial Infarction) or unstable angina
    • Eclampsia

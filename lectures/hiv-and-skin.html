Introduction

The skin is the interface between humans and their environment.

It is the largest organ in the body. It weighs an average of 4 kg and covers an area of 2 m2.

It acts as a barrier, protecting the body from harsh external conditions and preventing the loss of important body constituents, especially water.

The skin has two layers.

  • The outer is epithelial, the epidermis, which is firmly attached to, and supported by connective tissue in the underlying dermis. In between the epidermis and the dermis is the basement membrane.
  • The downward progression of the epidermis to the dermis is the rete pegs/ridges while the upwards attachment of the dermis to epidermis is dermal papillae. Beneath the dermis is loose connective tissue, the subcutis/hypodermis which usually contains abundant fat.

Embryology of the Skin

  • The skin has dual origin
  • A superficial layer, the epidermis, which develops from surface ectoderm
  • A deep layer, the dermis, which develops from the underlying mesenchyme
  • The embryo is covered initially by a single layer of ectodermal cells.
  • The epithelium starts to divide in the second month. The periderm and epitrichium are found on the surface. With further division in the basal layer, a third intermediate zone is formed. Finally, at the end of the fourth month, the four layers can be distinguished
  • The basal layer produces new cells. A thick spinous layer consists of large polyhedral cells containing fine tonofibrils. The granular layer contains small keratohyalin granules in its cells. The horny layer forming the tough scale-like surface of the epidermis is made up of dead cells containing keratin. Cells of the periderm are usually cast off during the second part of intrauterine life and can be found in amniotic fluid.
  • During the first three months, the epidermis is invaded by cells arising from neural crest; these cells synthesize melanin pigment, and they are transmitted to other cells of the epidermis by dendritic processes.
  • The dermis is derived from lateral plate mesoderm and dermatomes from somites. During the third and fourth months, the corium forms many irregular papillary structures, the dermal papillae. At birth, the skin is covered by a whitish plate, vernix caseosa, formed by secretions from sebaceous gland and degenerated epidermal cells and hair. This protects the skin against the macerating action of amniotic fluid.
  • Hair appears as a solid epidermal proliferation penetrating the underlying dermis. At their terminal ends, hair buds invaginate, and the invaginations, the hair papillae, are rapidly filled with mesoderm in which vessels and nerve endings develop. Cells in the center of the hair buds become spindle-shaped and keratinized, forming the hair shaft. All hair follicles follow a growth cycle: the anagen growth phase, catagen involution phase, telogen shedding phase. At any one time, most hairs, greater than 90%, will be in the anagen phase, which is three to five years for scalp hair. Grey hair is due to decreased tyrosinase activity in hair bulb melanocytes. White hair is due to the total loss of these melanocytes.

Nails are tough plates of hardened keratin which arise from the nail matrix, just visible as the moon-shaped lunula under the nail fold. It takes six months for a fingernail to grow out fully and one year for a toenail.

Structure and Functions of the Skin

The Epidermis

The epidermis is a stratified epithelium that arises from dividing basal keratinocytes, and the projection of the epidermis into the dermis forms rete ridges.

The struucture of the skin

The basal layer produces a variety of keratin filaments and desmosomal proteins, such as desmoglein and desmoplakin, which make up the cytoskeleton. This confers strength to the epidermis and prevents it from shedding off.

Higher up in the granular layer, complex lipids are secreted by keratinocytes, and this forms the intracellular lipid bilayer, which acts as a semipermeable skin barrier.

The upper cells, the stratum corneum, lose their nuclei and become surrounded by a tough impermeable envelope of various proteins, including loricrin, involucrin, filaggrin, and keratin.

Keratinocytes can secrete a variety of cytokines, interleukins, and gamma interferon. They play a role in specific immune function, cutaneous inflammation, and tissue repair. There is also an innate immune system in the skin, which includes neutrophils and macrophages, as well as antimicrobial peptides produced by keratinocytes called Î²-defensins and cathelicidins. These peptides are active against bacterial, viral, and fungal pathogens.

Other cells in the epidermis:

  • Melanocytes: Found in the basal layer, melanocytes secrete the pigment melanin, which protects against ultraviolet radiation. Racial differences in skin color are due to variation in melanin production and not melanocyte numbers.
  • Merkel cells: Also found in the basal layer, Merkel cells are numerous on fingertips and in the oral cavity, and they play a role in sensation.
  • Langerhans cells: These dendritic cells are found in the suprabasal layer and act as antigen-presenting cells.

Basement Membrane Zone

This is a complex proteinaceous structure consisting of type 4, type 7, and type 17 (BP 180) collagen, hemidesmosomal protein, integrin, and laminin. Collectively, they hold the skin together, keeping the epidermis firmly attached to the dermis.

×

Dermis

The dermis is mesodermal in origin and contains blood and lymphatic vessels, nerves, muscles, appendages such as sweat glands, sebaceous glands, and hair follicles. It also contains a variety of immune cells such as mast cells and lymphocytes. It is a matrix of collagen and elastin in a ground substance.

×

Sweat Glands

Eccrine sweat glands are found throughout the skin except on mucosal surfaces.

Apocrine sweat glands are found in axillae, anogenital areas, and the scalp and do not function until puberty.

The sweat glands and vasculature are involved in temperature control.

Sebaceous Glands

Sebaceous glands are inactive until puberty. They are responsible for secreting sebum or grease onto the skin surface through the hair follicle and are found in high numbers on the face and scalp.

Nerves

The skin is richly innervated. These fibers allow sensation of touch, pain, itch, vibration, and changes in temperature.

Hair

Hair arises from a downgrowth of epidermal keratinocytes into the dermis.

The hair shaft has an inner and outer root sheath, a cortex, and sometimes a medulla. The lower portion of the hair follicle consists of an expanded bulb that also contains melanocytes surrounding a richly innervated and vascularized dermal papilla. The hair regrows from the bulb after shedding.

There are three types of hair:

  • Terminal: medullated coarse hair, e.g., scalp, beard, and pubic hair.
  • Vellus: non-medullated fine downy hair seen on the face of women and in prepubertal children.
  • Lanugo: non-medullated soft hair on newborns, most marked in premature babies and occasionally in people with anorexia nervosa.

Nails

Nails are tough plates of hardened keratin that arise from the matrix of a nail fold.

Subcutaneous layer

The subcutaneous layer consists predominantly of adipose tissue as well as blood vessels and nerves. This layer provides insulation and acts as a lipid store.
